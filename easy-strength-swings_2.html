Goblet Squat Warmup
10–15 reps · grease the hinge pattern
0
/ 15 reps
Why: Dan John calls this the "cure" for movement problems. It teaches the squat pattern, opens the hips, and warms up the entire posterior chain at once. Do it with a light-to-moderate KB — this is prep, not training.
1
Hold a KB at chest height with both hands, one hand on each side of the handle. Feet shoulder-width or slightly wider, toes turned out about 30°.
2
Sit down between your knees — not back over your heels. Think of dropping straight down, not sitting back. Elbows come inside your knees at the bottom.
3
At the bottom, use your elbows to push your knees out slightly. Chest stays tall. Pause for a second and feel the stretch in your hips.
4
Stand back up by driving your feet through the floor. Squeeze your glutes at the top. That's one rep.

Six-Point Rocks
10 reps · reset the spine and nervous system
10
reps
Why: This is from Tim Anderson's Original Strength system — Dan John includes it in every warmup. Rocking reconnects your breathing with your movement, resets your nervous system, and activates your core before you load it.
1
Get on all fours — hands under shoulders, knees under hips. You're now on six points (2 hands, 2 knees, 2 feet).
2
Take a deep breath in. As you breathe out, slowly rock your hips back towards your heels, like you're trying to sit back. Let your arms stretch forward as you go.
3
Rock forward slowly back to the start position. Keep your head relaxed — don't look up, let your neck stay long.
4
Breathe naturally and move at a slow, controlled pace. The goal is smooth, rhythmic movement — not a stretch. 10 rocks total.

Head Nods ("Find Your Shoes")
10 reps · activate the reflexive core
10
reps
Why: The head and neck control the rest of the body through reflexive pathways. Nodding your head activates your deep neck flexors, which switches on your core — Dan John calls this one of his greatest training secrets.
1
Stand tall or sit on the edge of a bench. Shoulders relaxed, arms loose at your sides.
2
Tuck your chin gently — like you're making a slight double chin. Hold for 1 second. This is the "yes" nod. Dan John calls it "finding your shoes" because you're looking slightly down.
3
Return to neutral. Don't jerk or rush — slow and deliberate. You should feel a gentle activation in the front of your neck and your deep abs.
4
Repeat 10 times. Add a gentle side-to-side head rotation (look left, look right) for another 5 reps each way if you have time.

10 reps · thoracic extension and core reset
10

Why: Same concept as head nods but from the floor. Lying face down and nodding activates the posterior chain and gets the thoracic spine moving. It counters the forward-head posture most people carry from sitting.
1
Lie face down on the floor with your arms resting alongside your body or hands under your forehead. Completely relaxed.
2
Gently lift your head and tuck your chin — "find your shoes" — looking slightly down your body. Don't crane your neck up, just a gentle nod forward.
3
Lower your head back down. Breathe. That's one rep. You may feel a gentle lengthening through your spine.
4
10 reps. Move slowly. If your neck feels tight, this is normal — it means it needs it.

Egg Rolls
5 each side · spinal mobility and coordination
5+5
each side
Why: Egg rolls develop rotational movement of the spine and coordinate the upper and lower body. Dan John includes them because most training is front-to-back — rotation is the gap. They also develop the reflexive stability you need for heavy lifts.
1
Lie on your back. Pull your knees to your chest and hug them with both arms. Curl up like an egg. Tuck your chin gently.
2
Using your legs as a pendulum, gently roll to one side (say, your right). Your right elbow and right knee move towards each other. Roll far enough that your right hip touches the floor.
3
Roll back through centre and continue to the left side. Left elbow and left knee reach toward each other. That's one full rep.
4
5 rolls to each side. Keep your chin tucked throughout. Move slowly and feel your spine articulate. If one side is stiffer, spend a little extra time there.

Hip Flexor & Hamstring Stretch
30–45 sec each side · stretch what's stiff
30s
each side
Why: Dan John's principle: "stretch what's stiff, strengthen what's weak." For most people, the hip flexors and hamstrings are chronically tight from sitting. Loosening these before deadlifts and swings improves range of motion and protects the lower back.
1
Hip Flexor: Kneel on one knee (half-kneeling). Back knee on the floor, front foot forward. Shift your weight gently forward until you feel a stretch in the front of the back hip. Don't arch your lower back. Hold 30 seconds. Switch sides.
2
Hamstring: From the half-kneeling position, straighten your front leg and hinge forward from your hips — not your back — until you feel a stretch in the back of your front thigh. Hold 30 seconds. Switch sides.
3
Breathe steadily through both stretches. Don't bounce or force the range. The goal is relaxation, not pain.

The Rules
Never Miss. Never Grind.
Show up every session. Never miss a rep. Leave 2–3 in the tank every set. If you're questioning whether you'll make the rep, the weight is too heavy.
75 Real Swings
Dan John settled on 75 reps of real swings per session after testing 250 and 125. Not EMOM, not timed — just 75 quality reps. Sets of 10–15, rest when needed.
Carries: Never Repeat
The carry rotates every session: Farmer → Suitcase → Waiter → Rack. Never repeat the same carry session. Change load or distance each time too.
Add Load When It Feels Easy
Wait until a weight feels genuinely easy for two consecutive sessions. OHP/Deadlift: +2.5–5kg. Pull-ups: add belt weight. Patience is the mechanism.
